Pricing: Where the Real Difference Shows Up

Mailchimp's free plan is the most common reason people choose it first. Up to 500 contacts, 1,000 sends per month, zero cost. That sounds like a great deal until you realize what it does not include: scheduling, automation, A/B testing, or phone support. The free plan is stripped down to the point where you are really just using it as a send button with a basic editor.

When you grow past 500 contacts — which most active small businesses do within six months of serious email marketing — Mailchimp's pricing becomes complicated. The Essentials plan starts at $13 per month for 500 contacts but climbs quickly. At 2,500 contacts, you are paying $30 per month for Essentials. At 5,000 contacts, you are at $50 per month. That is before you add any advanced features, which require jumping to the Standard plan ($20 per month for 500 contacts, $45 per month for 2,500 contacts).

Constant Contact is simpler. The Lite plan starts at $12 per month for 500 contacts with unlimited sends. The Standard plan starts at $35 per month and adds A/B testing, more automation series, and advanced scheduling. At 2,500 contacts on the Standard plan, you pay $50 per month. At 5,000 contacts, around $80 per month. The pricing is higher at scale, but what you get at each tier is clearer and the support is included no matter which plan you are on.

For a small business owner sending one campaign per week to a list of 1,000 people, Constant Contact at $12 per month is the better value. You get live support, proper automation, and a deliverability rate above 97 percent. Mailchimp at that list size would cost you $20 to $30 per month once you need any of the features that make email marketing actually work.

Ease of Use: Which One Can You Actually Learn in an Afternoon?

Constant Contact was built for people who are not marketers. The editor loads fast, the drag-and-drop works intuitively, and the template library is sorted by industry — so a restaurant owner sees food-themed templates, and a retail shop sees promotional layouts. You can go from signing up to scheduling your first email in under 30 minutes without watching a single tutorial.

Mailchimp has more features at higher tiers, but the interface reflects that complexity. Audiences, Tags, Segments, Groups — these terms all mean slightly different things in Mailchimp's system, and getting them confused creates real problems when you try to send targeted campaigns. The editor is good, but the navigation assumes you already know what you are doing. First-time users frequently report confusion around where to find templates, how to set up a basic welcome email, and why their contacts appear to be counted twice.

If you have never used email marketing software before, Constant Contact will get you sending productive campaigns faster. If you already use digital tools regularly and enjoy learning new software, Mailchimp is manageable — but the learning investment is real.

Customer Support: The Feature Nobody Talks About Until They Need It

Here is a scenario that happens to small business owners all the time. You schedule an important email — a Black Friday sale, a product launch, a customer re-engagement campaign — and something goes wrong. The images are not loading. The list looks wrong. The send failed. It is 7 PM on a Thursday and you need help right now.

With Constant Contact, you call a phone number and speak to a real person. Their support hours are Monday through Friday 8 AM to 10 PM Eastern and Saturday 10 AM to 8 PM Eastern. They also offer live chat and an extensive knowledge base. Their average call wait time is under five minutes during business hours.

With Mailchimp, your support access depends on your plan. Free plan users get only email support — and responses can take 24 to 48 hours. Essentials and Standard plan users get email and chat support. Only Premium plan users get phone support, and that plan starts at $350 per month. For a small business owner paying $13 to $50 per month, there is no phone support option. If something breaks at a critical moment, you are in a chat queue or waiting for an email reply.

This is one of the clearest and most practical advantages Constant Contact has over Mailchimp for small business owners. Support is not a luxury feature. It is a basic requirement when real money is riding on a campaign going out correctly.

How to Get Started with Constant Contact in Five Steps

If you have decided Constant Contact is the right move, here is exactly how to get your first campaign out the door without wasting time.

  1. Sign up for the free trial. Go to Constant Contact's sign-up page and start a 60-day free trial. No credit card is required. Enter your business name and industry so the platform can pre-load relevant templates.
  2. Import your contact list. If you have an existing list from another platform, a spreadsheet, or a CRM, upload it as a CSV file. Constant Contact accepts most common formats and will flag any formatting issues automatically. If you are starting from zero, add contacts manually or connect a signup form to your website.
  3. Set up a welcome email automation. Before you send your first broadcast, go to the automation section and create a simple welcome email that triggers when someone joins your list. This single email — which takes about 15 minutes to set up — will work for you every day going forward without any additional effort.
  4. Write and design your first campaign. Pick a template that matches your industry, drop in your logo and brand colors, write your message, and preview it on both desktop and mobile. Keep the goal of your email to one clear action: a visit to your website, a redemption of an offer, or a reply to a question.
  5. Schedule and review your results. Send at an optimal time — Tuesday through Thursday mornings between 9 AM and 11 AM consistently perform well for small business audiences. After the send, check your open rate and click rate in the dashboard. A 20 to 25 percent open rate is healthy for most small business lists.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Constant Contact better than Mailchimp for small businesses?

For most small businesses, yes. Constant Contact offers phone and live chat support, simpler pricing, and better list management tools. Mailchimp has a free plan, but its feature restrictions and complicated billing structure make it harder to grow with. If you need reliable help when something goes wrong, Constant Contact wins. The support difference alone is worth the small cost difference at entry-level pricing.

Does Mailchimp have a free plan and is it worth using?

Mailchimp does offer a free plan limited to 500 contacts and 1,000 sends per month. It sounds appealing but removes key features like automation, A/B testing, and scheduling. Most small businesses outgrow it within a few months and face a steep price jump. The free plan is better as a trial than a long-term solution. If you want a genuine trial period, Constant Contact's 60-day free trial is a stronger option.

How much does Constant Contact cost per month?

Constant Contact starts at $12 per month for the Lite plan, which includes unlimited email sends to up to 500 contacts, basic automation, and live support. The Standard plan starts at $35 per month and adds scheduling, more automation, and A/B testing. Pricing scales with your list size, but remains predictable and straightforward. There are no surprise feature upgrades that require jumping to a more expensive plan to unlock basic functionality.

Which tool is easier to use for a first-time email marketer?

Constant Contact is easier for beginners. Its drag-and-drop editor is straightforward, its onboarding walkthrough takes under 10 minutes, and its template library is organized by industry. Mailchimp has a steeper learning curve, especially around audience segmentation and billing. If this is your first email tool, Constant Contact will get you sending faster and with fewer frustrations during setup. Most first-time users report being fully operational within one afternoon.

Can I switch from Mailchimp to Constant Contact without losing my list?

Yes. You export your contacts from Mailchimp as a CSV file, then import that file into Constant Contact. The process takes about 15 minutes. Constant Contact's support team can walk you through it live if you need help. You keep all your contact data, including names, email addresses, and custom fields. Campaign history does not transfer, but your list is fully portable. There is no technical barrier to making the switch.
